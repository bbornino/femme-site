Transition Tips and Tricks

This isn’t a comprehensive guide. It’s a set of hard-earned lessons from navigating a system that doesn’t always work the way it should.

Dealing with Insurance

Start here: the number on the back of your insurance card is almost never enough.

In my experience with Blue Shield of California, the front-line representatives had no idea what gender-affirming services like electrolysis or hair restoration even were—despite those services being required coverage in California. If I had stopped there, I would have been told “it’s not covered” and left it at that.

What actually made the difference was finding their internal gender care team (the name varies—“Gender Confirmation Team,” “Coordinator,” etc.). These teams are usually small, specialized groups whose only job is handling gender-affirming care. Once I reached them, the entire experience changed—they understood the process, the requirements, and how to navigate approvals.

If you take one thing from this: don’t stop at the general support line. Ask specifically if your insurer has a dedicated gender care coordinator or team—and be prepared to push to get there.

When I later switched to Medi-Cal and ended up with Health Net, even asking for that team was a challenge. Multiple front-line agents had no idea what I was referring to. It took going through several calls, escalating to managers, and repeatedly explaining what I needed before I was finally connected to the right place. In other words: even when the support exists, the system may not know how to route you to it.

There’s also a financial reality that doesn’t get talked about enough: even when something is “covered,” you may still have to front the cost. In my case, I paid out of pocket for hundreds of hours of electrolysis before being reimbursed later—and those reimbursements depended heavily on my plan at the time. When my coverage changed (state employment → Covered California → Medi-Cal), my copays and reimbursement structure changed with it.

If you’re in California and using Medi-Cal, be aware that not all carriers are equal—and even the better ones may still require significant effort to navigate.

Finally, escalation matters. When my surgeon pushed back against the insurance company—and eventually involved the state—coverage that had been denied for months was suddenly approved, resulting in over $30,000 being paid. That only happened because someone knew how to push the system.

The system can work—but it often doesn’t work passively. You may have to push, escalate, and insist on reaching the people who actually understand it.

Note: this is the primary driver of COST. Personally, I've had to front over $100k!

Selecting Healthcare Providers

Not all providers are equipped to handle transgender care—and that becomes very clear once you start navigating the system.

I was initially within UC Davis Health, a large and well-known medical system. On paper, that should have been ideal. In reality, while I had access to primary care and specialists, there wasn’t a consistent or modern approach to transgender care. Even as they began forming a gender-focused program, the methodologies I encountered were often rigid or outdated, and not aligned with my goals.

This is something I learned the hard way: provider size or reputation does not guarantee relevant expertise.

Who Should Be Directing Your Care

It’s critical to understand who is actually responsible for your treatment.

Based on my experience, your care should be anchored by a physician (MD) who specializes in transgender healthcare—typically in internal medicine or family practice.

The reason for this is not just the title—it’s the scope. The right MD doesn’t simply refer everything out. They understand how hormone therapy impacts the body as a whole and are comfortable managing care that overlaps with endocrinology, dermatology, and overall systemic health.

That means:

  • they can monitor and adjust treatment directly
  • they don’t rely on disconnected specialists for every decision
  • they treat your care as a complete system, not a series of unrelated parts

That distinction matters more than anything else.

Where NP/PA Providers Fit (and Where It Breaks Down)

Nurse practitioners (NPs) and physician assistants (PAs) can absolutely be part of good care—but only under the right structure.

  • NP/PA working within a specialized, MD-led transgender care team → can be effective and well-supported
  • NP/PA operating independently without that backing → much higher risk of outdated, rigid, or incorrect care

In my experience, the biggest problems came from situations where care decisions were being made without access to experienced oversight in transgender medicine. That’s where I ran into outdated assumptions, unnecessary barriers, and treatment limitations that didn’t reflect what’s actually possible.

If no one is clearly owning your care at a system level, that’s a problem.

The Turning Point: Finding a Specialized Provider

Everything changed when I found a primary care provider who specializes in transgender care.

Instead of being passed between departments, I had someone who:

  • understood hormone therapy in depth
  • adjusted treatment based on actual progress
  • approached care with current, evolving medical knowledge
  • had a trusted network of surgeons experienced in transgender procedures
  • knew how to navigate insurance effectively
That last point is critical: a knowledgeable provider doesn’t just treat you—they help you access the rest of the system.

One practical way to begin finding these providers is through networks built by specialists in the field. For example, Will Powers maintains a community and informal network of providers who focus on transgender care. While not exhaustive, resources like this can be a strong starting point for identifying clinicians who are more likely to be experienced and up to date.

Expect to Look Outside Your Area

One of the hardest lessons: truly competent care may not be local.

I live in the Sacramento area, yet the providers and surgeons who were actually experienced in transgender care were located in Southern California. That meant traveling—but it also meant finally receiving care that was aligned with my goals and based on current practices.

It’s not ideal, but in some cases, expanding your search geographically is what makes the difference.

Barriers and Gatekeeping

Another reality is that some systems create unnecessary barriers to care.

In my experience, this included:

  • being required to obtain multiple psychological evaluations without clear justification
  • inconsistent or contradictory requirements depending on the provider
  • being pushed through approval steps, only to later be told services weren’t offered

These kinds of processes can delay care significantly and create additional stress without improving outcomes.

If you encounter this, it’s worth seriously questioning whether that provider or system is the right fit.

What to Look For (and Red Flags)

Good signs:

  • A provider who regularly treats transgender patients (not “occasionally”)
  • Clear, thoughtful explanations of why a treatment approach is being used
  • Willingness to adjust care based on your goals and your body’s response
  • A referral network of surgeons and specialists they trust
  • Familiarity with navigating insurance for gender-affirming care
  • A sense that someone is taking ownership of your care as a whole

Red flags:

  • Rigid, one-size-fits-all protocols with no explanation
  • Outdated or absolute statements about what is or isn’t possible
  • Being passed between departments with no clear direction
  • Requirements for multiple approvals without a clear medical reason
  • Providers who say they “don’t offer” services only after making you go through extensive steps
  • Inability to explain how or why decisions are being made

Final Takeaway

The difference isn’t just credentials—it’s whether someone is taking responsibility for your care as a complete system, using current knowledge, and guiding you through the process.

When you find that, everything gets easier.

When you don’t, the system can become incredibly difficult to navigate.
