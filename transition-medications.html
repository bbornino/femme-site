Transition Medications (MtF)

To put it plainly, there are no medications designed specifically for MtF transition. Instead, we are prescribed drugs originally developed for other conditions: enlarged prostate medications (such as finasteride or dutasteride), blood pressure medications used for heart and kidney conditions (such as spironolactone), a blood pressure drug later repurposed for hair growth (minoxidil), hormone-suppressing therapies used in prostate cancer and precocious puberty (GnRH analogs), and, when providers are willing to prescribe them, estrogen-based medications and sometimes progesterone.

Finasteride and Dutasteride

Many of us experience androgenic hair loss, commonly referred to as male pattern baldness. This is strongly influenced by dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a hormone derived from testosterone that can shrink hair follicles in genetically susceptible individuals. To reduce further loss, we often use medications like finasteride or dutasteride, which reduce DHT levels and can slow or partially reverse follicle miniaturization. Some clinicians prefer dutasteride over finasteride in their practice, and may choose not to prescribe finasteride at all. This is typically based on their clinical experience with DHT suppression strength, response consistency, and perceived tolerability differences between the two medications, rather than a universal guideline difference.

However, though these medications can prevent further loss, they do not promote regrowth.

Minoxidil

For regrowth, minoxidil is often used. Originally developed as a blood pressure medication, it was later found to promote hair growth as a side effect. Its exact mechanism is not fully understood, but it appears to extend the growth phase of hair follicles and improve follicle function, which is why it can help stimulate regrowth in areas where follicles are still viable. It is available as both a topical treatment and an oral medication. While originally studied and marketed primarily for male pattern baldness, topical minoxidil is now widely used for both male- and female-pattern hair loss, with labeling and approved indications evolving across different formulations and dosages over time.

Minoxidil comes in two forms: a topical solution and an oral pill. The topical solution is applied directly to the scalp, where it acts locally at the hair follicles. In practice, application can be more difficult when there is significant hair coverage, since getting the solution onto the scalp evenly can take more effort. The oral form is systemic and can affect the entire body, which means it may carry different side effect considerations compared to topical use. Both forms can be effective, with selection depending on individual response and tolerance.

If you have any issues with your hair, start taking these two kinds of medications soon to prevent any long lasting damages of testosterone and DHT on your hair!

Monotherapy with Estradiol

Once we are ready for the effects (and side effects) of estrogen, we can start taking estradiol. In many care models, it is prescribed alongside anti-androgens such as spironolactone. This approach is widely used and well-established, though it is not the only available method. Some clinicians instead use higher-dose estradiol alone (monotherapy), depending on patient response and clinical judgment.

With combination therapy, spironolactone is used to reduce the effects of testosterone, allowing lower doses of estradiol to achieve feminizing effects. This approach can be effective, but it may also introduce additional medication-specific side effects that need to be managed.

Monotherapy approaches focus on estradiol dosing and suppression of testosterone without additional anti-androgens, and require careful monitoring of hormone levels over time. Clinicians may adjust treatment based on estradiol levels, testosterone suppression, and individual response.

A good provider will monitor hormone levels regularly and adjust dosing to balance effectiveness and side effects.

Progesterone and Breast Development

Lastly, breast development is often discussed in terms of Tanner stages, which describe the progression of puberty-related physical changes. In some clinical practices, providers use these stages as a guide when considering whether to introduce additional hormones. For example, some clinicians choose not to prescribe progesterone until a certain level of breast development (often around Tanner stage 3) is reached, based on their clinical judgment and interpretation of available evidence.

The role of progesterone in feminizing hormone therapy is still not fully established in the medical literature. Some people report changes in breast fullness or shape, while others do not notice significant differences. Because of this, its use varies significantly between providers and treatment approaches. Breast development itself is primarily driven by estradiol exposure over time and is highly individual in outcome.

Next Steps

If your provider is unwilling to discuss or explain these options, it's worth seeking a second opinion:

  • If you’re starting to lose your hair, it’s time to start finasteride or dutasteride to slow further loss, and minoxidil to support regrowth.
  • Consider estradiol monotherapy when appropriate (to reduce anti-androgen side effects)
  • Actually consider prescribing progesterone as an option
